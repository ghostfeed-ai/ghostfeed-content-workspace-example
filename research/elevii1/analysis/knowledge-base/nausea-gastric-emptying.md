# Nausea & Slowed Gastric Emptying

## Overview
Retatrutide (Reta) heavily affects the digestive system by significantly delaying gastric emptying—meaning food stays in the stomach and digestive tract for much longer than normal. While this mechanism is highly effective for keeping users full, reducing "food noise," and facilitating a calorie deficit, it is also the primary cause of gastrointestinal side effects. If a user eats heavy, greasy foods, consumes too much volume at once, or increases their medication dose too quickly, the slowed digestion causes severe nausea, stomach cramps, bloating (often referred to as "Reta Belly"), acid reflux, constipation, diarrhea, and sulfur burps. 

## What The Creator Says
The creator emphasizes that dealing with extreme nausea, repulsion to food, or all-day stomach discomfort is *not* a normal or necessary part of the process—it is a major "red flag" that your dosage is too high or your diet is incorrect. Retatrutide is not intended to shut off your appetite completely. If it is impossible to eat a normal meal without feeling sick, the dose needs to be lowered. Nausea and digestive distress are overwhelmingly caused by user errors: rushing the titration schedule, eating highly processed or high-fat meals that the stomach can no longer process quickly, or cramming too much food volume into a single sitting.
